1
Wrong typeface — Bricolage Grotesque used instead of Playfair Display
P1 Global Typography
What
The font loaded is Bricolage Grotesque — a variable grotesque. The PRODUCT.md spec calls for Playfair Display (serif, headings) + Plus Jakarta Sans (body/UI). The brand register is explicitly "editorial, sharp, serif-anchored." Bricolage is a tech startup font.
Why it matters
Typography carries the brand register. "The Crystallizer" should feel like a senior advisor's letterhead — precise, with gravitas. Bricolage Grotesque reads as a product designer's portfolio. The brand promise and the visual presentation are in direct conflict.
Recommended fix
Load Playfair Display for the hero title, section headings (.how-title, .contact-headline, .about-title), and the nav logo. Keep a strong sans for body — Plus Jakarta Sans or the current Bricolage limited to body weight only. The hero title especially should be Playfair at weight 700–800.
6
Hero badge feels like a product availability pill — wrong register
P2 Hero Anti-pattern
What
An indigo pill badge with a pulsing dot and "Positioning & Go-to-Market" in uppercase. This pattern signals "status: online" or "new feature" in SaaS dashboards. It's the hero-availability-badge cliche.
Why it matters
The PRODUCT.md anti-references explicitly ban SaaS landing page patterns. This is one of the most specific SaaS tells: the pulsing availability dot before the hero headline. It undermines the consulting gravitas immediately.
Recommended fix
Replace with a simple .t-label eyebrow — all-caps sans, no border, no background, no dot. Just "Positioning & Go-to-Market" tracked in gold or indigo at 10px. Or remove it entirely — the hero headline says everything needed.
7
Tagline undersized relative to the headline — hierarchy collapses
P2 Hero Typography
What
The hero title hits 64–96px. The tagline "You know what you've built. I make sure everyone else does too." sits at 17–20px. Below that, the lede is 15px. The three text elements are visually indistinguishable in weight — all read as body copy beneath the headline.
Why it matters
The tagline is the brand's most important sentence. It should feel like a subheading, not a caption. The current scale ratio between headline and tagline is approximately 4:1 — the tagline disappears in the shadow of the giant title.
Recommended fix
Raise tagline to 22–26px, weight 500, tighter tracking. Consider making it italic in Playfair for a quotation-like quality. Let there be only two text sizes below the headline, not three competing ones at similar scale.
8
Primary CTA button style contradicts the editorial brand
P2 Hero CTA
What
The "Let's talk" button is a filled indigo rounded rectangle with a box shadow — standard SaaS button style. Combined with the ghost link "See my work," the hero action area is visually noisy and generic.
Why it matters
A referred, senior-level prospect notices when the UI doesn't match the consultant's claimed positioning. "I distill" and "I clarify" are the brand verbs — yet the interface shouts with generic product-page patterns.
Recommended fix
Use a minimal outlined or text-based CTA. Or: a single text link with a long em-length underline that animates on hover. Reserve filled buttons exclusively for the contact section dark background where they read as high-contrast.
9
Photo background shape and name tag feel decorative and corporate
P2 Hero Visual design
What
The photo sits on an indigo-tinted background shape with a floating name tag pill. This is a portfolio-template pattern — used on every Figma community UI kit. The name is also redundant: it appears in the nav logo and the hero title implies it.
Why it matters
The photo is there to humanize. Wrapping it in decorative chrome reduces the human and increases the template-feel. Senior consultants don't have floating name tags on their portrait photos.
Recommended fix
Remove the name tag entirely. Replace the indigo background shape with a flat, slightly warm-tinted surface (a rectangle at offset) or simply let the photo stand alone with a natural shadow. Let the face hold the composition.

Marquee strip is a missed opportunity — three statements deserve to be read, not scrolled past
P2 Statement Strip Content strategy
What
The three statements ("The world is full of great products...", "Clarity is a competitive advantage...", "If you can't explain what you do...") are the sharpest copy on the page. They loop in a marquee where most users will only catch one fragment.
Why it matters
The brand promise is built on clarity and directness. Burying the clearest statements in an autoplay loop is structurally ironic — it prioritizes visual motion over the message. Referred visitors who scroll quickly will miss the best lines.
Recommended fix
Replace the marquee with a dark full-bleed static section. Display all three statements stacked or in a 3-column grid. Large, readable, with generous whitespace. Let the dark background do the visual differentiation work. The motion budget is better spent elsewhere.

3
Gold accent completely absent — indigo carries all accent work it was never designed for
P1 Global Color strategy
What
The PRODUCT.md color system specifies --gold: #b8975a as the accent — used for the crystal motif facets throughout. In the actual code, --gold is never defined as a token. The indigo family handles every accent role: badges, CTAs, pillar numbers, hover states, border highlights, and the crystal SVG fill.
Why it matters
Gold is the crystal's defining visual property. It's what makes the motif feel premium and distinct — not just "a gem shape in brand color." Replacing it with indigo flattens the crystal into a generic icon and makes the color strategy read as "consulting firm that chose purple." The gold gives warmth, rarity, and the physical quality of a real crystal.
Recommended fix
Define --gold: oklch(68% 0.09 78) (OKLCH equivalent of #b8975a) and use it for: all crystal SVG fills, the t-label eyebrow text, the about-section rule accent, and the section divider bars. Keep indigo for interactive elements (buttons, focus rings, links). Let gold be the decorative/brand color, indigo be the UI color.

P2 Contact CTA copy
What
The primary CTA button displays "hagaiheshes@gmail.com" as the full button label inside an indigo pill. It links to Gmail's compose view, which fails for users not using Gmail on this device.

Footer text contrast falls below WCAG AA on the very dark background
P2 Footer Accessibility
What
Footer text uses oklch(44% 0.01 265) on var(--dark-bg2) = oklch(7% 0.02 265). Computed approximate contrast ratio: ~3.2:1. WCAG AA minimum for 11px text (small text) is 4.5:1. The footer copy and nav links fail this threshold.
Why it matters
Even if the footer is secondary content, WCAG AA compliance is a baseline expectation. The copyright and nav links need to be legible — especially for users in bright light conditions on phone screens.
Recommended fix
Raise footer text to oklch(56% 0.012 265) or higher to achieve ~4.5:1 contrast. Alternatively, increase text size to 12px (where the AA threshold drops to 3:1 for large text). Both are simple token adjustments.

Systemic Patterns — Root Causes

Pattern 1: Font specification drift

PRODUCT.md specifies Playfair Display + Plus Jakarta Sans. The implementation uses Bricolage Grotesque exclusively. This isn't a typo — someone made a deliberate font switch. The result is that the brand's serif identity, which was the central typographic decision, never made it into the build. Every heading, title, and display element is missing its intended register.

Pattern 2: Indigo system overextension

The color system was designed as "Restrained — one accent." The gold token for the crystal, and indigo for UI interactions. What shipped: indigo is used for crystal fills, badge backgrounds, badge text, button fill, button shadows, pillar numbers, label eyebrows, hover border effects, focus rings, and scroll progress animation. Gold is never referenced once. The token system was defined correctly but the implementation ignored the gold half of the spec entirely.

Pattern 3: SaaS anti-pattern infiltration

The PRODUCT.md anti-references explicitly ban SaaS patterns. Yet the site contains: a pulsing-dot availability badge (hero), a filled-indigo rounded CTA button (nav and hero), a logo grid with grayscale-to-color hover effect, and a scroll progress indicator (crystal frames). Each individual element is defensible; as a combined pattern, they read exactly as the anti-references described. The consultant who "can't afford to be misunderstood" is currently presenting as a SaaS product page.

Pattern 4: Section structural monotony

With one exception (the statement strip), every section uses the same structural template: t-label eyebrow, heading, body copy, on near-white background, separated by hairline borders. The page has visual egalitarianism when it needs visual hierarchy. A single dark-bg pillar section or a typographically dominant "How I Work" section would create the rhythm the scroll experience needs.

What's Working — Keep and Replicate

+
Contact section dark treatment is the strongest section on the page. The dark background, large headline with italic accent, and the crystal SVG ghost — this section has the energy and presence the rest of the page is missing. The visual language here should inform the rest of the redesign.
+
Statement marquee copy is excellent. "Clarity is a competitive advantage. Most companies don't have it." — this line alone justifies Hagai's fee. The copy quality is above the page design quality. The words are doing work the layout should be amplifying.
+
The crystal SVG motif is well-executed geometrically. The faceted polygon with variable opacity facets reads as a real gem under light, not a flat icon. It is the right visual metaphor and the geometry is intentional. The problem is color (indigo instead of gold), not form.
+
Accessibility fundamentals are mostly solid. Focus-visible rings, aria-labels on icon-only buttons, semantic HTML landmarks, mobile touch targets at 44px, reduced-motion support, passive scroll listeners. This is a well-built base that just needs design polish on top.
+
Hero tagline copy is sharp and human: "You know what you've built. I make sure everyone else does too." This is the best single sentence on the site and it belongs at a larger scale. It should be the section title, not a 20px afterthought below a 96px logo name.
